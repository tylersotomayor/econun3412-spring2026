\section*{Problem 1 \points{30}}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Problem 1: Concealed Weapons Laws and Violent Crime}

\begin{problem}
Some U.S.\ states have enacted laws that allow citizens to carry concealed weapons. These laws are known as ``shall-issue'' laws because they instruct local authorities to issue a concealed weapons permit to all applicants who are citizens, mentally competent, and have not been convicted of a felony (some states have some additional restrictions). Proponents argue that, if more people carry concealed weapons, crime will decline because criminals are deterred. Opponents argue that crime will increase because of accidental or spontaneous use of the weapon. In this exercise, you will analyze the effect of concealed weapons laws on three different categories of crimes: violent crimes; robberies (such as the robbery of a convenience store); and murder (many of which are spontaneous acts of passion).

Data file and the description of variables are at the end of this problem set.
Estimate
\end{problem}

\begin{enumerate}

\item[(i)]
\begin{problem}{\points{3}}
A regression of $\ln(\textit{vio})$ against \textit{shall}.
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The data are from \texttt{handguns.dta}, a balanced panel of 51 U.S.\ states (including D.C.) over 1977--1999 ($n = 1{,}173$), with crime rates from the Bureau of Justice Statistics \citep{BJS}. This dataset was used in the influential debate between \citet{LottMustard1997} and \citet{DonohueAyres2003} on shall-issue laws and crime. The baseline specification regresses the natural log of the violent crime rate on the shall-issue indicator alone, using heteroskedasticity-robust (Eicker--Huber--White) standard errors. In Stata:

\begin{verbatim}
    use "handguns.dta", clear
    gen ln_vio = ln(vio)
    reg ln_vio shall, robust
\end{verbatim}

The estimated equation is:
\[
\boxed{
\widehat{\ln(vio)}_i
=
\underset{(0.019)}{6.135}
\;-\;
\underset{(0.048)}{0.443}\;\textit{shall}_i
}
\]

\noindent
$n = 1{,}173$, \quad $R^2 = 0.0866$.

The coefficient on \textit{shall} is $-0.443$ and is statistically significant at the 1\% level ($t = -9.32$, $p < 0.001$). Without any controls, states with shall-issue laws in effect have, on average, approximately 44.3\% lower violent crime rates than states without such laws. However, this naive estimate likely suffers from omitted variable bias (OVB), as states that adopt shall-issue laws may differ systematically from non-adopting states in ways that also affect crime. See Table~\ref{tab:p1_reg_i} in the Appendix for the full regression output.
\end{solution}

\item[\textbf{(ii)}]
\begin{problem}{\points{3}}
A regression of $\ln(\textit{vio})$ against \textit{shall}, \textit{incarc\_rate}, \textit{density}, \textit{avginc}, \textit{pop}, \textit{pb1064}, \textit{pw1064}, and \textit{pm1029}.
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The multiple regression adds seven state-characteristic control variables that capture cross-state differences in incarceration policy, urbanization, income, population size, and demographic composition. In Stata:

\begin{verbatim}
    reg ln_vio shall incarc_rate density avginc pop ///
        pb1064 pw1064 pm1029, robust
\end{verbatim}

The estimated equation is:
\begin{align*}
\widehat{\ln(vio)}_i
&= \underset{(0.609)}{2.982}
\;-\;
\underset{(0.035)}{0.368}\;\textit{shall}_i
\;+\;
\underset{(0.0002)}{0.0016}\;\textit{incarc\_rate}_i \\
&\quad +\;
\underset{(0.014)}{0.027}\;\textit{density}_i
\;+\;
\underset{(0.007)}{0.001}\;\textit{avginc}_i
\;+\;
\underset{(0.003)}{0.043}\;\textit{pop}_i \\
&\quad +\;
\underset{(0.020)}{0.081}\;\textit{pb1064}_i
\;+\;
\underset{(0.010)}{0.031}\;\textit{pw1064}_i \\
&\quad +\;
\underset{(0.012)}{0.009}\;\textit{pm1029}_i
\end{align*}

\noindent
$n = 1{,}173$, \quad $R^2 = 0.5643$, \quad $\bar{R}^2 = 0.5613$.

The coefficient on \textit{shall} is $-0.368$ and remains highly significant ($t = -10.59$, $p < 0.001$). The inclusion of controls substantially raises $R^2$ from 0.087 to 0.564, indicating that the control variables explain a large share of the cross-state variation in violent crime. See Table~\ref{tab:p1_reg_ii} in the Appendix for the full regression output.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}{\points{8}}
Interpret the coefficient on \textit{shall} in regression (ii). Is this estimate large or small in a ``real-world'' sense?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The coefficient on \textit{shall} in regression (ii) is $\hat{\beta}_{\textit{shall}} = -0.368$. Because the dependent variable is $\ln(\textit{vio})$ and \textit{shall} is a binary indicator, this coefficient has a log-level (semi-elasticity) interpretation: holding all other regressors fixed, the presence of a shall-issue law is associated with a change of $-0.368$ in the log of the violent crime rate. By the standard log-point approximation, this corresponds to approximately a $100 \times (-0.368) = -36.8\%$ reduction in the violent crime rate (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~8). More precisely, using the exact transformation $\exp(\hat{\beta}) - 1 = \exp(-0.368) - 1 \approx -0.308$, the estimated reduction is about 30.8\%.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Real-world magnitude.} Given that the sample mean of the violent crime rate is approximately 503 incidents per 100,000 population, a 30--37\% reduction would imply roughly 155--185 fewer violent crimes per 100,000 residents. This is an extraordinarily large effect. To put it in context, the entire decline in the U.S.\ violent crime rate from its 1991 peak to 1999 was on the order of 30\%, and this decline is attributed to a wide range of factors (increased incarceration, demographic shifts, economic growth, policing strategies, among others). An effect of this magnitude is economically very large and suggestive of remaining omitted variable bias --- it would be difficult for a single policy change to account for a reduction comparable to the entire national crime decline of the 1990s. The pooled OLS estimate, even after controlling for observables, likely still reflects confounding from unobserved differences between adopting and non-adopting states (e.g., political culture, historical crime trends, law enforcement practices) that are correlated with both the passage of the law and with crime rates. The original and influential study by \citet{LottMustard1997} reported large crime-reducing effects of shall-issue laws using similar cross-state variation, but these estimates have been widely critiqued for sensitivity to specification choices \citep{DonohueAyres2003, NRC2005}.

\begin{center}
\fbox{\parbox{0.85\textwidth}{\centering
$\hat{\beta}_{\textit{shall}} = -0.368 \;\Longrightarrow\; \approx 30.8\%$--$36.8\%$ reduction; economically large, suggestive of remaining OVB.
}}
\end{center}
\end{solution}

\begin{reflection}
The log-level interpretation of a dummy variable coefficient follows from the general result that for $\ln Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 D + \mathbf{X}'\boldsymbol{\gamma} + u$, the exact percentage change associated with $D$ switching from 0 to 1 is $100 \times [\exp(\hat{\beta}_1) - 1]\%$ (see \citealt{Wooldridge2019}, \S6.2; \citealt{StockWatson2020}, \S8.1). The $100 \times \hat{\beta}_1$ approximation works well for small $|\hat{\beta}_1|$ but becomes less accurate as $|\hat{\beta}_1|$ grows. For $\hat{\beta}_1 = -0.368$, the discrepancy between the approximation ($-36.8\%$) and the exact figure ($-30.8\%$) is notable, illustrating why the exact calculation is preferred when the coefficient is large.

The economically large magnitude of the OLS estimate motivates the use of panel data methods in Problem 2, where state and year fixed effects will be introduced to control for unobserved heterogeneity. This is the central methodological lesson of this exercise: cross-sectional OLS can be severely biased when unobserved confounders are present, and fixed effects estimation provides a way to address time-invariant omitted variables.
\end{reflection}

\item
\begin{problem}{\points{8}}
Does adding the control variables in regression (ii) change your conclusions about the effect of a shall-carry law, relative to regression (i)?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Adding the seven state-characteristic control variables changes the magnitude of the estimated effect but does not change the qualitative conclusion. In regression (i), the coefficient on \textit{shall} is $-0.443$ ($\text{SE} = 0.048$), while in regression (ii) it becomes $-0.368$ ($\text{SE} = 0.035$). Both estimates are negative and highly statistically significant at conventional levels.

The coefficient shrinks by about 17\% in absolute value, from $-0.443$ to $-0.368$, when the controls are added. This attenuation is consistent with positive omitted variable bias in regression (i): states without shall-issue laws tend to have characteristics associated with higher crime (e.g., higher population density, larger black population share, higher incarceration rates), and controlling for these factors removes part of the spurious negative correlation between \textit{shall} and $\ln(\textit{vio})$.

However, the estimated effect remains very large---a 30--37\% reduction in violent crime is economically substantial and difficult to attribute to a single policy change. The fact that the estimate barely changes (from $-0.443$ to $-0.368$) despite a large increase in $R^2$ (from 0.087 to 0.564) suggests that the included controls, while explaining much of the variation in crime, do not adequately address the confounding that generates the large negative \textit{shall} coefficient. There are likely important omitted variables that are correlated with both shall-issue adoption and crime rates, which the observable controls cannot capture. This motivates the use of state fixed effects in Problem 2 to control for all time-invariant state-specific factors.

\begin{center}
\fbox{\parbox{0.85\textwidth}{\centering
Qualitative conclusion unchanged; magnitude decreases modestly: $-0.443 \to -0.368$.
}}
\end{center}
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}{\points{8}}
Suggest a variable which varies across states but plausibly varies little, or not at all, over time, and which plausibly could cause omitted variable bias in regression (ii).
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
A strong candidate is the \textbf{prevailing gun culture or attitudes toward firearms ownership} in each state. This variable reflects deeply rooted historical, cultural, and geographic factors---such as whether a state has a frontier heritage, rural traditions of hunting, or a longstanding culture of individual gun ownership---that vary substantially across states but change very slowly (if at all) over a 23-year panel.

Gun culture plausibly satisfies both conditions required for omitted variable bias (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~6): it must be correlated with the included regressor (\textit{shall}) and with the dependent variable ($\ln(\textit{vio})$), conditional on the other controls.

First, states with a stronger gun culture are more likely to pass shall-issue laws, because both the political demand for such legislation and the legislative willingness to enact it are higher where gun ownership is culturally normalized. This implies $\text{Corr}(\textit{gun culture}, \textit{shall}) > 0$.

Second, gun culture is plausibly correlated with violent crime rates through multiple channels. States with deep gun cultures may have higher rates of gun ownership, which could increase or decrease crime depending on one's theoretical priors. More importantly, such states also tend to differ in other hard-to-measure ways (e.g., social norms about conflict resolution, rural vs.\ urban composition beyond what \textit{density} captures, quality of law enforcement). These factors may independently affect crime rates.

Because gun culture is positively correlated with \textit{shall} and plausibly negatively correlated with crime (rural, gun-culture states tend to have lower violent crime rates for reasons beyond just gun laws), omitting it would bias $\hat{\beta}_{\textit{shall}}$ downward (more negative), making shall-issue laws appear more effective at reducing crime than they truly are. This is a form of negative omitted variable bias (since $\text{Corr}(\textit{gun culture}, \textit{shall}) > 0$ and the omitted variable is negatively correlated with the error term from the perspective of inflating the negative coefficient). This variable is approximately time-invariant within the sample period, which is precisely the type of confounding that state fixed effects are designed to eliminate.

\begin{center}
\fbox{\parbox{0.85\textwidth}{\centering
Gun culture / attitudes toward firearms\\
(varies across states, nearly time-invariant).
}}
\end{center}
\end{solution}

\begin{reflection}
The omitted variable bias formula for the simple case is
\[
\text{Bias}(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_2 \cdot \frac{\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2)}{\text{Var}(X_1)},
\]
where $X_2$ is the omitted variable and $\beta_2$ is its true coefficient (\citealt{Wooldridge2019}, \S3.3; \citealt{StockWatson2020}, \S6.1). This exercise is designed to motivate panel data fixed effects: variables that are roughly constant within each state over time are exactly the type of confounders that entity (state) fixed effects absorb. The ``within'' estimator differences out all time-invariant state characteristics, whether observed or unobserved, eliminating OVB from such sources.
\end{reflection}

\end{enumerate}

\begin{solution}
The five specifications estimated for each dependent variable are:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(1)] Pooled OLS of $\ln(Y)$ on \textit{shall} only, with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors.
\item[(2)] Pooled OLS adding the seven state-characteristic control variables (\textit{incarc\_rate}, \textit{density}, \textit{avginc}, \textit{pop}, \textit{pb1064}, \textit{pw1064}, \textit{pm1029}), with robust SEs.
\item[(3)] Adding 50 state fixed effect dummies (with one state as the omitted reference category), plus controls, with robust SEs. State FE absorb all time-invariant unobserved state-level heterogeneity. Identification of $\hat{\beta}_{\textit{shall}}$ now comes from \emph{within-state} variation over time, which in practice is driven by states that changed their shall-issue status during 1977--1999.
\item[(4)] Adding 22 year fixed effect dummies (with one year as reference) to specification (3), with robust SEs. Year FE absorb aggregate time trends common to all states (e.g., national crime trends, federal policy changes, macroeconomic cycles). This is the canonical two-way fixed effects (TWFE) model (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~10).
\item[(5)] Same as (4), but with standard errors clustered at the state level via \texttt{vce(cluster stateid)}. Clustering allows for arbitrary serial correlation (autocorrelation) in the error term within each state over time, as well as heteroskedasticity, producing HAC-robust inference (\citealt{Arellano1987}; \citealt{StockWatson2020}, \S10.5).
\end{itemize}

The Stata code for the full set of regressions (taking \textit{ln\_vio} as an example) proceeds as follows:

\begin{verbatim}
    * Generate log dependent variables
    gen ln_vio = ln(vio)

    * Create state and year dummies
    tabulate stateid, generate(st)
    tabulate year, generate(yr)

    * Column (1): shall only, robust SEs
    reg ln_vio shall, robust

    * Column (2): + controls, robust SEs
    reg ln_vio shall incarc_rate density avginc pop ///
        pb1064 pw1064 pm1029, robust

    * Column (3): + state FE, robust SEs
    reg ln_vio shall incarc_rate density   ///
        avginc pop pb1064 pw1064 pm1029    ///
        st2-st51, robust
    testparm st2-st51  // F-test: state FE

    * Column (4): + state FE + year FE
    reg ln_vio shall incarc_rate density   ///
        avginc pop pb1064 pw1064 pm1029    ///
        st2-st51 yr2-yr23, robust
    testparm st2-st51  // F-test: state FE
    testparm yr2-yr23  // F-test: year FE

    * Column (5): + clustered SEs
    reg ln_vio shall incarc_rate density   ///
        avginc pop pb1064 pw1064 pm1029    ///
        st2-st51 yr2-yr23,                 ///
        vce(cluster stateid)
    testparm yr2-yr23  // F-test: year FE
\end{verbatim}

This illustrates the importance of accounting for serial correlation in panel data, as emphasized by \citet{BertrandDufloMullainathan2004}. \begin{reflection}
The dramatic change in the \textit{shall} coefficient across specifications illustrates the core logic of fixed effects estimation. The pooled OLS estimator exploits both \emph{between-state} and \emph{within-state} variation, but the between-state variation is contaminated by unobserved state-level confounders. The within (fixed effects) estimator uses only within-state variation over time, effectively comparing each state to itself before and after the law change, thereby eliminating all time-invariant confounding.

The $F$-statistics for state fixed effects are extremely large (e.g., 210--309 for violent crime), confirming that states differ enormously in their baseline crime rates---differences that the pooled regressions attribute partly to \textit{shall}. The year fixed effects are also highly significant, reflecting common national crime trends (the crime wave of the late 1980s and the subsequent decline). By including both sets of fixed effects, the TWFE model identifies the \textit{shall} effect from the \emph{timing} of law adoption relative to other states, after removing both state-level means and common time shocks.

The increase in standard errors from column (4) to column (5) demonstrates why clustering is essential in panel data. When errors are serially correlated within states, conventional robust SEs understate the true sampling uncertainty, leading to spuriously small $p$-values. Clustering at the entity level (\citealt{Arellano1987}; \citealt{BertrandDufloMullainathan2004}; \citealt{StockWatson2020}, \S10.5) corrects this by allowing arbitrary within-cluster correlation.
\end{reflection}

\section*{Problem 3 \points{40}}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Problem 3: Stock Market Performance and Firm Returns}

\begin{problem}
In order to determine the effects of stock market performance on individual firm's performance, you collect a daily frequency data on firm's performance for $n$ sample of publicly listed companies for the period of January 1, 2021 -- January 1, 2026 on a (trading) daily frequency.
\end{problem}

\begin{enumerate}

\item
\begin{problem}{\points{8}}
What measures of firm performance would you include in an equation? What are some of the timing issues?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The most natural measure of firm performance in the context of stock market analysis is the \textbf{daily log return}, defined as
\[
r_{it} = 100 \times \bigl[\ln(P_{it}) - \ln(P_{i,t-1})\bigr],
\]
where $P_{it}$ is the adjusted closing price of firm $i$ on trading day $t$. The log-difference formulation is the standard econometric approach to measuring percentage changes (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~14): applying a log transformation to the price level and then first-differencing yields a variable that is (i) approximately equal to the simple percentage return $(P_{it} - P_{i,t-1})/P_{i,t-1}$ for small daily changes, (ii) additive over time (multi-day log returns are the sum of daily log returns), and (iii) approximately stationary---an essential property for valid regression inference, since levels of stock prices are typically non-stationary (exhibiting unit roots). The adjusted close price is preferred over the raw close because it accounts for stock splits, dividends, and other corporate actions that mechanically alter the price level without reflecting true performance changes.

Alternative measures one might consider include: (i) \textbf{abnormal returns} (the firm's return minus the expected return from an asset pricing model), which isolate the firm-specific component of performance; (ii) \textbf{simple percentage returns}, $100 \times (P_{it} - P_{i,t-1})/P_{i,t-1}$, which are nearly identical to log returns for daily data but lack the additive property; and (iii) \textbf{trading volume} or \textbf{market capitalization changes}, though these capture liquidity and size rather than performance per se.

Several timing issues arise with daily financial data. First, there is the question of \textbf{non-synchronous trading}: if some stocks close trading at slightly different times (e.g., due to trading halts or different exchange closing times), daily returns may not reflect the same information set, introducing measurement error. Second, stock prices may react to market-wide news with a \textbf{lag}---particularly for less liquid, smaller-cap stocks---which would argue for including lagged market returns alongside the contemporaneous market return in the regression (the \citealt{Dimson1979} correction). Third, prices respond to information released \textbf{between trading days} (overnight and weekend effects), so the daily close-to-close return conflates intraday movements and overnight reactions. Fourth, announcements or events (earnings releases, Federal Reserve decisions) typically occur at specific times; if the event falls after market close, the price impact appears on the following trading day, creating a one-day lead-lag alignment issue. Finally, the choice of whether to use \textbf{calendar time} or \textbf{trading-day time} matters: regressions should be run on trading days only, as weekends and holidays introduce spurious gaps.

\[
\boxed{
r_{it} = 100 \times \bigl[\ln(P_{it}) - \ln(P_{i,t-1})\bigr] \quad \text{(daily log return using adjusted close)}
}
\]
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}{\points{8}}
What other factors might you control for in the equation?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Beyond the overall stock market return (e.g., the S\&P 500 return), several additional factors may influence individual firm stock returns and should be considered as controls, drawing on the asset pricing literature and the econometrics of panel data.

At the \textbf{firm level}, one might control for: (i) \textbf{firm size} (measured by log market capitalization), since small firms tend to exhibit higher average returns and greater sensitivity to market shocks than large firms---this is the ``size effect'' documented by \citet{FamaFrench1993}; (ii) \textbf{book-to-market ratio}, capturing the ``value premium'' whereby high book-to-market (``value'') firms earn higher average returns than low book-to-market (``growth'') firms; (iii) \textbf{momentum} (past cumulative returns over the prior 3--12 months), since stocks that have performed well recently tend to continue outperforming in the short run (\citealt{JegadeeshTitman1993}); and (iv) \textbf{trading volume or liquidity measures}, as illiquid stocks may exhibit larger price swings and lagged responses to market movements.

At the \textbf{macroeconomic and market level}, useful controls include: (v) \textbf{interest rate changes} (e.g., changes in the federal funds rate or the 10-year Treasury yield), since monetary policy affects discount rates and hence stock valuations differently across sectors; (vi) \textbf{credit spreads} (the difference between corporate bond yields and risk-free rates), which capture changes in perceived default risk and economic uncertainty; (vii) \textbf{exchange rate fluctuations}, particularly relevant for firms with international operations; and (viii) \textbf{sector-specific indices or returns}, since firms within the same industry are exposed to common sector-level shocks (e.g., oil prices for energy firms, interest rates for banks) that are distinct from market-wide movements.

At the \textbf{time-series level}, controlling for (ix) \textbf{day-of-week effects} (the ``Monday effect'') and (x) \textbf{lagged dependent variables} (lagged firm returns) can account for well-documented calendar anomalies and return persistence or mean reversion. In a panel context, \textbf{firm fixed effects} ($\alpha_i$) absorb all time-invariant firm characteristics (sector, size category, geographic location, management quality) and \textbf{time fixed effects} ($\delta_t$) absorb all market-wide daily shocks (news, sentiment, macroeconomic releases), making them powerful catch-all controls.

\begin{center}
\fbox{\parbox{0.85\textwidth}{\centering
Key controls: firm FE, time FE, size, book-to-market, momentum, interest rates, sector returns.
}}
\end{center}
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}{\points{8}}
Write an equation that allows you to estimate the effects of the overall stock market performance on the percentage change in firm's stock price. How would you estimate this equation? Why would you choose this method?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The baseline equation for estimating the effect of overall market performance on the percentage change in a firm's stock price in a panel setting is:
\[
\boxed{
\Delta \ln(P_{it}) = \alpha_i + \beta \cdot \Delta \ln(P_{mt}) + u_{it}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, n, \;\; t = 1, \ldots, T
}
\]
where $\Delta \ln(P_{it}) = \ln(P_{it}) - \ln(P_{i,t-1})$ is the daily log return of firm $i$ on trading day $t$ (the log-difference measure of percentage change), $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$ is the daily log return of the S\&P 500 index on day $t$, $\alpha_i$ is a firm-specific fixed effect (intercept), and $u_{it}$ is the idiosyncratic error term. The parameter of interest is $\beta$, which measures the average sensitivity of firm stock returns to market-wide movements---the ``market beta'' in the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) tradition (\citealt{Sharpe1964}; \citealt{Lintner1965}).

This equation would be estimated using \textbf{fixed effects (within) estimation}. In Stata, this is implemented via the \texttt{xtreg} command with the \texttt{fe} option:

\begin{verbatim}
    xtset firm_id trading_date
    xtreg log_ret_firm log_ret_market, ///
        fe vce(robust)
\end{verbatim}

The fixed effects estimator is chosen for several reasons. First, the panel structure of the data (multiple firms observed over the same trading days) naturally calls for a panel estimator rather than pooled OLS. Second, different firms have different average returns reflecting persistent differences in risk, industry, growth prospects, and other characteristics; these firm-specific mean returns are captured by $\alpha_i$. Including firm fixed effects absorbs this intercept heterogeneity, ensuring that the estimate of $\beta$ is not contaminated by cross-firm differences in average return levels (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~10). The within transformation demeans each variable by its firm-specific mean, so $\beta$ is identified from the day-to-day co-movement of firm and market returns around their respective means (\citealt{Wooldridge2019}, Ch.~14).

Third, the within estimator is numerically equivalent to the Least Squares Dummy Variable (LSDV) approach---including a full set of $n$ firm dummies---but is computationally more efficient when $n$ is large. Fourth, using heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors (or clustered standard errors at the firm level) ensures valid inference even if the variance of $u_{it}$ differs across firms or exhibits serial correlation within firms.

An extended specification that also controls for aggregate time effects would be:
\[
\Delta \ln(P_{it}) = \alpha_i + \delta_t + \beta \cdot \Delta \ln(P_{mt}) + u_{it}
\]
where $\delta_t$ are time (day) fixed effects. However, note that including day fixed effects alongside $r_{mt}$ creates a near-perfect multicollinearity problem, since $r_{mt}$ is constant across firms on any given day and is thus almost perfectly spanned by the day dummies. In practice, one would either include day FE \emph{without} $r_{mt}$ (letting the day dummies absorb all common daily shocks) or include $r_{mt}$ without day FE. The choice depends on the research question: if the goal is to estimate $\beta$, one uses $r_{mt}$ directly; if the goal is to control for all common daily shocks without parametric restriction, one uses day FE.
\end{solution}

\begin{reflection}
This equation is essentially the market model from empirical finance, a single-factor version of the CAPM \citep{Sharpe1964, Lintner1965}. The key econometric insight is that firm fixed effects absorb all time-invariant firm characteristics (sector, size, management quality, geographic exposure), allowing $\beta$ to be estimated from within-day co-movement of firm and market returns without permanent cross-firm differences confounding the estimate.
\end{reflection}

\bigskip
\textbf{(ii) Controlling for Time Fixed Effects.} To additionally control for time (trading-day) fixed effects, one would augment the model with a full set of day-specific dummy variables:
\[
\Delta \ln(P_{it}) = \alpha_i + \delta_t + \beta \cdot \Delta \ln(P_{mt}) + u_{it}
\]
where $\delta_t$ represents the fixed effect for trading day $t$. In Stata, one could either generate day dummies manually or use \texttt{i.stata\_date} factor notation:

\begin{verbatim}
    reg log_ret_firm log_ret_market ///
        i.firm_id i.stata_date,     ///
        vce(robust)
\end{verbatim}

However, there is an important identification problem. The market return $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$ varies only across time, not across firms---it takes the same value for all $n$ firms on any given trading day $t$. Including a full set of day fixed effects $\delta_t$ (one dummy per trading day) would absorb all variation that is common to all firms on each day, which is exactly the variation that $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$ exploits for identification. In the extreme, $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$ is a deterministic function of $\delta_t$ (since it is perfectly collinear with the day dummies), and the coefficient $\beta$ would be unidentified.

In practice, if we want to control for aggregate daily shocks while still estimating $\beta$, we have several options. One approach is to include \textbf{time fixed effects but drop $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$}, letting the day dummies absorb all common daily variation; this controls for market movements non-parametrically but does not produce an estimate of $\beta$. A second approach is to use \textbf{year or month fixed effects} rather than daily fixed effects, which absorb low-frequency common trends while leaving enough within-period variation in $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$ for $\beta$ to be identified. A third approach is to include additional \textbf{time-varying aggregate controls} (such as changes in the VIX volatility index, interest rate changes, or oil price changes) alongside $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$, rather than saturating the model with day dummies.

The simplest implementation for time FE that avoids the collinearity issue would be:

\begin{verbatim}
    * Year FE (coarse; avoids collinearity)
    gen year = year(dofC(stata_date))
    xtreg log_ret_firm log_ret_market ///
        i.year, fe vce(robust)
\end{verbatim}

\begin{center}
\fbox{\parbox{0.85\textwidth}{\centering
Day FE and $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$ are collinear; use coarser time FE (year/month) or drop $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$.
}}
\end{center}
\end{solution}

\begin{reflection}
The collinearity between time fixed effects and aggregate regressors is a fundamental identification issue in panel data. When the regressor of interest varies only across time (like $\Delta \ln(P_{mt})$), entity fixed effects are fine (they remove cross-sectional variation that the regressor does not exploit), but time fixed effects remove the very source of variation that identifies the coefficient. This is a concrete illustration of the general principle that fixed effects ``eat'' the variation they span: including $T$ time dummies leaves only cross-sectional variation within each period, and if the regressor is identical across entities within each period, there is no variation left.

The recommended approach depends on the research question. If the goal is to estimate market beta ($\beta$), use firm FE without day FE (or with coarse time FE). If the goal is to isolate firm-specific shocks from all aggregate factors, use two-way FE (firm + day) but recognize that $\beta$ is not separately identified from $\delta_t$ (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~10).
\end{reflection}

\end{enumerate}

\Delta \widehat{\textit{Deaths}} = 0.0530 \times 885 \approx 46.9.
\]
The 95\% CI is $[0.54 \times 0.0296 \times 885,\; 3.04 \times 0.0296 \times 885]$:
\[
\boxed{95\%\;\text{CI} = [14.2,\; 79.7] \text{ additional deaths}.}
\]
Higher income is associated with more traffic deaths, plausibly because higher income leads to more driving.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
How should standard errors be clustered in the regressions in columns (2) through (7)?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Standard errors should be \textbf{clustered at the state level}. Each state is observed over multiple years, and the error terms for a given state are likely serially correlated (e.g., a state with unusually dangerous roads in 1984 will probably still have dangerous roads in 1985). Clustering at the state level allows for arbitrary within-state correlation over time and for heteroskedasticity across states. This is the approach recommended by \citet{BertrandDufloMullainathan2004} and implemented in all columns (2)--(7) of Table 10.1.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
How should minimum drinking age be included in the regressions? Should it enter as a continuous variable or as a series of indicator variables? Be specific about the information you use to assess this question.
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The minimum drinking age should enter as a \textbf{series of indicator (dummy) variables} rather than as a continuous variable. Table 10.1 provides direct evidence to assess this. In columns (4) and (5), the drinking age enters as three dummies (age 18, 19, 20) with age 21 as the omitted category. The coefficients ($0.03$, $-0.02$, $0.03$ in column 4; $-0.01$, $-0.08$, $-0.10$ in column 5) do not follow a monotonic linear pattern. In column (6), the drinking age enters as a single continuous variable with coefficient $0.00$ ($\text{SE} = 0.02$), completely insignificant. The $F$-test for joint significance of the three dummies yields $F = 0.35$ ($p = 0.786$) in column (4) and $F = 1.41$ ($p = 0.253$) in column (5). Indicator variables are preferred because they allow the effect of each age level to be estimated freely without imposing linearity.
\end{solution}

\end{enumerate}

\begin{reflection}
Exercise 10.1 reinforces several core panel data concepts. Parts (a)--(c) develop the skill of translating regression coefficients into real-world predictions: multiply the coefficient by the change in the regressor, then scale by the population. The log-level specification in part (c) requires computing $\Delta \ln(\textit{income}) = \ln(1.03)$ rather than simply using $0.03$---a common mistake. Part (d) reinforces that clustering at the entity level is essential to account for serial correlation (\citealt{Arellano1987}; \citealt{BertrandDufloMullainathan2004}). Part (e) illustrates the ``continuous vs.\ dummies'' modeling choice: indicator variables are more flexible when the relationship may be nonlinear, and the $F$-test for joint significance tests whether the variable matters at all.
\end{reflection}

\subsection*{SW Exercise 10.5}

\begin{problem}
Consider the model with a single regressor. This model also can be written as
\begin{align*}
Y_{it} = \beta_0 &+ \beta_1 X_{1,it} + \delta_2 B2_t + \cdots + \delta_T BT_t \\
&+ \gamma_2 D2_i + \cdots + \gamma_n Dn_i + u_{it},
\end{align*}
where $B2_t = 1$ if $t = 2$ and $0$ otherwise, $D2_i = 1$ if $i = 2$ and $0$ otherwise, and so forth.
How are the coefficients $(\beta_0, \delta_2, \ldots, \delta_T, \gamma_2, \ldots, \gamma_n)$ related to $(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_T)$?
\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
The entity and time fixed effects model can be written in two equivalent ways. The first uses entity-specific intercepts $\alpha_i$ and time-specific intercepts $\lambda_t$:
\[
Y_{it} = \beta_1 X_{1,it} + \alpha_i + \lambda_t + u_{it}, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n, \quad t = 1, \ldots, T.
\]
The second uses a common intercept $\beta_0$ and deviations from the reference categories via dummy variables:
\begin{align*}
Y_{it} = \beta_0 &+ \beta_1 X_{1,it} + \delta_2 B2_t + \cdots + \delta_T BT_t \\
&+ \gamma_2 D2_i + \cdots + \gamma_n Dn_i + u_{it},
\end{align*}
where entity 1 and time period 1 are the omitted reference categories. To derive the mapping, evaluate the second equation for each combination:

For entity $i = 1$, time $t = 1$: all dummies are zero, so $E[Y_{1,1} \mid X] = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1,1,1}$. From the first formulation, this equals $\alpha_1 + \lambda_1 + \beta_1 X_{1,1,1}$. Therefore:
\[
\beta_0 = \alpha_1 + \lambda_1.
\]

For entity $i = 1$, time $t = s$ (where $s \geq 2$): only $B_s = 1$, so $E[Y_{1,s} \mid X] = \beta_0 + \delta_s + \beta_1 X_{1,1,s} = (\alpha_1 + \lambda_1) + \delta_s + \beta_1 X_{1,1,s}$. From the first formulation, this equals $\alpha_1 + \lambda_s + \beta_1 X_{1,1,s}$. Equating:
\[
\delta_s = \lambda_s - \lambda_1, \qquad s = 2, \ldots, T.
\]

For entity $i = j$ (where $j \geq 2$), time $t = 1$: only $D_j = 1$, so $E[Y_{j,1} \mid X] = \beta_0 + \gamma_j + \beta_1 X_{1,j,1} = (\alpha_1 + \lambda_1) + \gamma_j + \beta_1 X_{1,j,1}$. From the first formulation, this equals $\alpha_j + \lambda_1 + \beta_1 X_{1,j,1}$. Equating:
\[
\gamma_j = \alpha_j - \alpha_1, \qquad j = 2, \ldots, n.
\]

Collecting these results:
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\beta_0 &= \alpha_1 + \lambda_1 \\
\delta_s &= \lambda_s - \lambda_1, \quad s = 2, \ldots, T \\
\gamma_j &= \alpha_j - \alpha_1, \quad j = 2, \ldots, n
\end{aligned}
}
\]

The common intercept $\beta_0$ absorbs the fixed effects of the reference entity (entity 1) and the reference period (period 1). Each time dummy coefficient $\delta_s$ measures the \emph{deviation} of period $s$ from the reference period. Each entity dummy coefficient $\gamma_j$ measures the \emph{deviation} of entity $j$ from the reference entity. The slope coefficient $\beta_1$ is identical in both formulations, as it captures the within-entity, within-period effect of $X$ on $Y$ regardless of the parameterization.
\end{solution}

\begin{reflection}
This exercise clarifies an important but often confusing point about the two equivalent parameterizations of the TWFE model. In the ``$\alpha_i + \lambda_t$'' formulation, one entity and one time effect must be normalized (e.g., set to zero) to avoid perfect multicollinearity with the intercept. Stata's \texttt{i.entity i.time} notation automatically drops one category from each set. The dummy-variable formulation with $\beta_0$ makes the normalization explicit: entity~1 and period~1 serve as the reference categories, and all other effects are measured relative to them. The key insight is that only the \emph{differences} $\alpha_j - \alpha_1$ and $\lambda_s - \lambda_1$ are identified---the absolute levels of the fixed effects are not separately identified from the intercept. This does not affect estimation of $\beta_1$, which is the parameter of interest (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~10; \citealt{Wooldridge2019}, Ch.~14).
\end{reflection}

\subsection*{SW Empirical Exercise E10.2}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{SW Empirical Exercise E10.2: Income and Democracy}

\begin{problem}
Do citizens demand more democracy and political freedom as their incomes grow? That is, is democracy a normal good? The data file \textbf{Income\_Democracy} contains a panel data set from 195 countries for the years 1960, 1965, \ldots, 2000. The data set contains an index of political freedom/democracy (\textit{Dem\_ind}) for each country--year, together with log per capita income (\textit{Log\_GDPPC}) and demographic controls (lagged five years).

\begin{verbatim}
  use "income_democracy.dta", clear
  xtset code year, delta(5)
\end{verbatim}
\end{problem}

\begin{enumerate}

\item
\begin{problem}
Is the data set a balanced panel? Explain.
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
A \textbf{balanced panel} requires every cross-sectional unit to be observed in every time period (\citealt{StockWatson2020}, \S10.1). Tabulating observations by year (\texttt{tab year}) reveals that $N$ ranges from 95 in 1960 to 195 in 2000, so the panel is \textbf{unbalanced}. New countries enter the dataset over time as former colonies gain independence and post-Soviet states appear after 1990.
\[
\boxed{\text{The panel is unbalanced: } N \text{ ranges from 95 (1960) to 195 (2000).}}
\]
\end{solution}

\item The index of political freedom/democracy is labeled \textit{Dem\_ind}.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]

\item
\begin{problem}
What are the minimum and maximum values of \textit{Dem\_ind}? What are the mean and standard deviation? What are the 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, and 90th percentiles?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Using \texttt{summarize dem\_ind, detail}:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lclc}
\toprule
Min & 0 & Mean & 0.481 \\
p10 & 0 & Std.\ Dev. & 0.372 \\
p25 & 0.167 & p75 & 0.833 \\
p50 (median) & 0.500 & p90 & 1 \\
Max & 1 & & \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The distribution is roughly bimodal: many country-years cluster near 0 (autocracies) or 1 (democracies), with the median at 0.5.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
What is the value of \textit{Dem\_ind} for the United States in 2000? Averaged over all years?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The United States scores 1.0 in 2000 with an all-years average of 0.986 (slightly below 1 because early years capture pre--civil-rights-era restrictions).
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
What is the value of \textit{Dem\_ind} for Libya in 2000? Averaged over all years?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Libya scores 0.0 in 2000 with an all-years average of 0.109 under the Gaddafi regime.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
List five countries with an average value of \textit{Dem\_ind} greater than 0.95; less than 0.10; and between 0.3 and 0.7.
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Computing country-level means via \texttt{bysort country: egen avg\_dem = mean(dem\_ind)}:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Avg.\ $> 0.95$ & Avg.\ $< 0.10$ & Avg.\ $\in [0.3, 0.7]$ \\
\midrule
Australia & Afghanistan & Argentina \\
Canada & China & Brazil \\
Denmark & Cuba & Chile \\
Norway & Iraq & Mexico \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The first group consists of long-standing, stable democracies. The second consists of persistently authoritarian regimes. The third contains ``hybrid'' or transitional regimes.
\end{solution}

\end{enumerate}

\item Regress \textit{Dem\_ind} on \textit{Log\_GDPPC} with clustered standard errors.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]

\item
\begin{problem}
How large is the estimated coefficient? Is it statistically significant?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
The pooled OLS regression $\texttt{Dem\_ind}_{it} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \texttt{Log\_GDPPC}_{it} + u_{it}$ with standard errors clustered by country (\texttt{reg dem\_ind log\_gdppc, cluster(code)}) yields $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.236$ ($\text{SE} = 0.012$, $t = 19.91$, $p < 0.001$). The coefficient is highly statistically significant: a one-unit increase in log GDP per capita (roughly a 170\% increase in income) is associated with a 0.236-point increase in the democracy index on a 0-to-1 scale.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
If per capita income increases by 20\%, by how much is \textit{Dem\_ind} predicted to increase? Construct a 95\% CI. Is the increase large or small?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
A 20\% increase in income corresponds to $\Delta \ln(\textit{GDPPC}) = \ln(1.20) \approx 0.1823$. The predicted change is
\[
\Delta \widehat{\texttt{Dem\_ind}} = 0.236 \times 0.1823 \approx \boxed{0.043}.
\]
The 95\% CI for $\hat{\beta}_1$ is $[0.212, 0.259]$, so the 95\% CI for $\Delta \texttt{Dem\_ind}$ is $[0.039, 0.047]$. On a 0-to-1 scale, this is \textbf{relatively small}: a substantial 20\% income increase closes less than 5\% of the gap between a fully autocratic and fully democratic country.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
Why is it important to use clustered standard errors? Do the results change without clustering?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Clustering is essential because panel observations within each country are serially correlated: a country that is more democratic than predicted in 1980 is likely still so in 1985. Without clustering, standard errors are too small ($\text{SE} = 0.0086$ vs.\ $0.012$ clustered), leading to inflated $t$-statistics and spuriously precise confidence intervals. The point estimate is unaffected---only the standard errors change.
\end{solution}

\end{enumerate}

\begin{reflection}
The pooled OLS estimate of $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.236$ is large and highly significant, suggesting that richer countries are substantially more democratic. However, this regression exploits both between-country and within-country variation, and the between-country component is likely contaminated by time-invariant confounders. The clustering discussion reinforces \citet{BertrandDufloMullainathan2004}: without clustering, standard errors are biased downward by about 27\% here, which could lead to over-rejection of the null in weaker settings.
\end{reflection}

\item Fixed effects analysis:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]

\item
\begin{problem}
Suggest a time-invariant omitted variable that could bias the regression in (c).
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
A strong candidate is a country's \textbf{colonial/legal origin} (e.g., British common law vs.\ French civil law). This is essentially fixed over time, varies across countries, and is correlated with both income (former British colonies tend to be richer) and democracy (they inherited parliamentary institutions and common-law protections). Omitting it biases the pooled OLS coefficient upward. Other valid examples include geography/climate, ethnic fractionalization, and religious composition.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
Estimate with country fixed effects. How do your answers to (c) change?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
With country fixed effects (\texttt{xtreg dem\_ind log\_gdppc, fe cluster(code)}), the coefficient drops dramatically from 0.236 to $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.084$ ($\text{SE} = 0.032$, $t = 2.66$, $p = 0.009$)---a 64\% reduction. The effect of a 20\% income increase falls to $0.084 \times 0.1823 \approx 0.015$. Most of the pooled OLS correlation was driven by between-country differences, not within-country income growth causing democratization.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
Exclude Azerbaijan and rerun. Do the results change? Why or why not?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Excluding Azerbaijan has \textbf{no effect} on the results ($\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.084$, $n$ drops from 958 to 957). Azerbaijan has only one observation (year 2000), so the within estimator---which identifies $\beta_1$ from over-time variation---assigns it zero weight. When a country has only one observation, its demeaned values are identically zero: $\tilde{y}_{it} = y_{it} - \bar{y}_i = 0$. Any country observed in only one period is automatically irrelevant to the FE estimator.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
Suggest a time-varying omitted variable constant across countries.
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
A natural candidate is a \textbf{global democratization wave}---the international political ``climate'' in each period. Decolonization in the 1960s, the third wave of democratization in the 1980s--90s, and the end of the Cold War all affected democracy worldwide. These global forces also coincided with income growth trends, creating omitted variable bias. The fix is time fixed effects (year dummies).
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
Estimate with country and time fixed effects. How do your answers change?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
With two-way FE (\texttt{xtreg dem\_ind log\_gdppc i.year, fe cluster(code)}), the coefficient drops further to $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.054$ ($\text{SE} = 0.042$, $t = 1.26$, $p = 0.209$). The 95\% CI is $[-0.030, 0.137]$, which \textbf{includes zero}. The effect of a 20\% income increase is now $0.054 \times 0.1823 \approx 0.010$---less than one percentage point and statistically insignificant. The year $F$-test yields $F = 5.73$ ($p < 0.001$), confirming that time effects are jointly significant.
\end{solution}

\item
\begin{problem}
Include demographic controls. How do the results change?
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Adding demographic controls (five age-distribution shares, average years of education, log population) further attenuates the coefficient to $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.025$ ($\text{SE} = 0.054$, $t = 0.47$, $p = 0.642$). The 95\% CI is $[-0.082, 0.132]$. The $F$-test for joint significance of all demographic controls yields $F = 1.44$ ($p = 0.21$)---not jointly significant. The sample drops from 958 to 680 due to missing education data. The bottom line is unchanged: once entity and time FE are accounted for, income has no significant effect on democracy.
\end{solution}

\end{enumerate}

\begin{reflection}
The progressive attenuation of $\hat{\beta}_1$ from 0.236 to 0.025 as controls are added is the single most important takeaway. It mirrors exactly the pattern in Problems 1--2, where the shall-issue coefficient shrank from $-0.443$ to $-0.028$. In both cases, pooled OLS is severely biased by time-invariant confounders, and the within estimator eliminates this bias. The Azerbaijan example illustrates that entities observed in only one period have zero within-variation and contribute nothing to the FE estimator---a fundamental feature of the within transformation.
\end{reflection}

\item
\begin{problem}
What conclusions do you draw about the effects of income on democracy?
\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
The progressive results are summarized below:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{llcc}
\toprule
Specification & Controls & $\hat{\beta}_1$ & Significant? \\
\midrule
Pooled OLS & --- & 0.236 & Yes ($p < 0.001$) \\
$+$ Time FE & --- & 0.235 & Yes ($p < 0.001$) \\
$+$ Entity FE & --- & 0.084 & Yes ($p = 0.009$) \\
$+$ Entity \& Time FE & --- & 0.054 & No ($p = 0.209$) \\
$+$ Entity \& Time FE & Demographics & 0.025 & No ($p = 0.642$) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The strong cross-country correlation between income and democracy ($\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.236$ in pooled OLS) is largely spurious, driven by time-invariant confounders such as colonial history, geography, and culture. Once country fixed effects are included, the coefficient drops by 64\%, and once time fixed effects are added, it becomes statistically insignificant. In the most demanding specifications, the effect is small, insignificant, and its confidence interval includes zero.

\begin{center}
\fbox{\parbox{0.85\textwidth}{\centering
Democracy does not appear to be a ``normal good'' in the causal sense. The income--democracy correlation reflects common historical causes, not a direct causal channel from economic development to political liberalization.
}}
\end{center}

This conclusion is consistent with \citet{StockWatson2020}, Ch.~10, and the influential findings of \citet{AcemogluEtAl2008}, who argue that the income--democracy relationship reflects joint determination by historical institutional development rather than a causal ``modernization'' effect.
\end{solution}

\begin{reflection}
The overarching lesson of E10.2 is that \emph{correlation is not causation}, and panel data methods provide a powerful toolkit for distinguishing the two. The between-country correlation reflects centuries of divergent institutional development---colonial legacies, legal traditions, geographic endowments---that simultaneously determined both income levels and political institutions. Fixed effects estimation strips away these confounders by focusing on within-country changes over time, revealing that the causal effect of income growth on democratization is, at best, very small. This finding has profound implications for development policy: promoting economic growth alone is unlikely to trigger political liberalization without complementary institutional reforms.
\end{reflection}

\end{enumerate}
